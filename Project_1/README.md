**1. Define the following with examples from the dataset:**



**=>** **Types of data: Numerical \& Categorical**



**Numerical Data:** This refers to data that represents numbers and can be measured or counted.

Example: Household Income (₹75,720.7) and Age of Household Head (50.76).



**Types of Numerical Data :** Discrete : Countable numbers, Ex. Number of children

&#x09;  		  Continuous : Measurable quantities with decimals, Ex. Height, Weight



**Categorical Data:** This refers to data that represents groups or labels.

Example: Region (Urban/Rural) or Education Level (Graduate/Post-Grad).



**Types of Categorical Data :** Nominal **:** No specific order, Ex. Male/Female, Red/Blue

&#x09;		    Ordinal : Ordered categories, Ex. Low/Medium/High, Ratings



**=> Types of Statistics: Descriptive vs. Inferential**



**Descriptive Statistics :** Descriptive Statistics is the branch of statistics that focuses on summarizing, organizing, and presenting raw data in a meaningful way.



**Inferential Statistics :** Inferential Statistics is the branch of statistics that allows us to make predictions or draw conclusions about a larger group (called a population) by analyzing a smaller sample of data taken from it.



**=> What is Descriptive Statistics?**



It is a branch of statistics that focuses on summarizing the essential characteristics of a dataset through numerical calculations, graphs, or tables to make the data patterns easy to understand.



**2. Explain the difference between:**



**=> Mean, Median, Mode**



**MEAN (Average) :** The mean is the sum of all values divided by the number of values.

&#x09;	 Used when values are evenly distributed and there are no extreme outliers.



**MEDIAN (Middle Value) :** The median is the middle value in a sorted list.

&#x09;		Best when data has outliers or is skewed.



**MODE (Most Frequent Value) :** The mode is the value that appears most frequently.

&#x09;		     Useful for categorical data or when finding most popular choice.



**=> Range, Variance, Standard Deviation**



**Range :** The difference between the maximum and minimum value in the dataset.



**Variance :** Variance shows how far each value in the dataset is from the mean, on average.

&#x09;   It’s the average of the squared differences from the mean.



**Standard Deviation :** The square root of the variance.

It shows how much, on average, the values deviate from the mean, in original units (not squared).



**=> 3. Explain the following terms with formulas:**

###### 

**Gaussian Distribution :** A Gaussian distribution (or Normal distribution) is a bell-shaped curve that shows how data is distributed.

where the mean, median, and mode are all equal.



**Log Normal Distribution :** A Log-normal distribution is a continuous probability distribution of a random variable whose logarithm is normally distributed.



**3-Sigma Rule or Empirical Rule :** +- 1 Standard Deviation : 68%

&#x09;			 +- 2 Standard Deviation : 95%

&#x09;			 +- 3 Standard Deviation : 99.7%



**Percentiles :** A percentile indicates the value below which a given percentage of observations fall.

&#x09;      Example: The 80th percentile means 80% of the data lies below that value.



**Quartiles :** Specific percentiles that divide the data into four equal parts: Q1 (25%), Q2 (Median,50%), and Q3 (75%).



Five Number Summary : The Five Number Summary provides a quick overview of the spread and center of

&#x09;	      a dataset.

&#x09;	      Minimum : The smallest value

&#x09;	      Q1 : First Quartile (25th percentile)

&#x09;	      Q2 (Median) : Second Quartile (50th percentile)

&#x09;	      Q3 : Third Quartile (75th percentile)

&#x09;	      Maximum : The largest value



**Skewness :** Skewness is a statistical measure that describes the asymmetry of a data distribution around its mean. It helps identify if the data is symmetrically distributed or tilted (skewed) towards one side.



**Kurtosis :** Kurtosis measures the "tailedness" of a distribution—how heavy or light the tails are compared to a normal distribution. It tells us how likely it is to get outliers.
